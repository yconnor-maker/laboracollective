Opening

The Economics of Reproductive Restriction Are Not an Externality. They Are the Point.

There is a woman in Texas right now who cannot afford the $1,250 it would cost her to travel to New Mexico for an abortion. She is not a policy variable. She is a person making a calculation that the system has made on her behalf without her consent — and the outcome of that calculation will reshape her economic trajectory, and her children's, for the next 18 years. This report is about that calculation, scaled to 556,000 women, to $133 billion, to an insurance market that is about to evict 21 million people.

The economic literature on reproductive restriction is unusually consistent: restricting abortion access does not reduce economic cost. It transfers cost — from the healthcare system and public policy budgets to individual women, their families, and the employers who depend on their workforce participation. The aggregate numbers are not incidental side effects of ideological policy. They are the mechanism by which the restriction operates.

Understanding the economic architecture of reproductive restriction is not optional for anyone advising campaigns, operating clinics, or providing care in 2025. The money is the story.

The Insurance Cliff

114%: The Premium Shock That Will Hit 21 Million Americans in January 2026

The Affordable Care Act's enhanced premium tax credits — expanded by the American Rescue Plan in 2021 and extended by the Inflation Reduction Act — expire at the end of 2025. Unless Congress acts, every household currently receiving enhanced subsidies will face dramatic premium increases starting January 1, 2026.

The average increase is 114% — from $888 to $1,904 per month for a family of four. In states where the ACA marketplace is the primary coverage option for self-employed, gig workers, and small business owners, this is not a marginal adjustment. It is an eviction notice from the insurance market. The households most affected are not the poorest — those retain Medicaid. The households most affected are the working middle class earning between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level, who relied on enhanced subsidies to make premiums affordable.

⚠ Critical Alert

January 1, 2026 is the cliff date. Patients currently covered through ACA marketplace plans with enhanced subsidies need to know their coverage status now — before open enrollment closes and before their January bill arrives. Healthcare providers, particularly those in states with high marketplace enrollment (Florida, Texas, North Carolina, Georgia), should expect a wave of coverage lapses, delayed care-seeking, and payment disruption beginning in Q1 2026.

In Your District

West Virginia is the highest-risk state for the premium cliff: high marketplace enrollment, low Medicaid expansion, high proportion of self-employed and small business workers. A 60-year-old couple earning $85,000 — too much for Medicaid, too much for maximum subsidies — faces premiums jumping from approximately $600 to $4,500 per month. That couple exists in every swing district in the Appalachian corridor, the rural South, and the Mountain West. They are not abstract beneficiaries. They are persuadable voters who just had their healthcare ripped away.

Medicaid Contraction

4.6 to 5.3 Million People Losing Coverage: The Largest Medicaid Purge in 60 Years

The One Big Beautiful Budget Act (OBBBA) includes Medicaid work requirements that will purge between 4.6 and 5.3 million people from Medicaid coverage. This is not a projected figure from advocacy organizations. It is the Congressional Budget Office's own scoring of Section 71113 of the bill.

The mechanism: adults between 19 and 64 who are not disabled, not pregnant, and not primary caregivers must document 80 hours per month of qualifying work, education, or community service to maintain Medicaid eligibility. The documentation requirement alone — not the actual work requirement — is the primary driver of disenrollment. Analyses of Arkansas's 2018 work requirement (later struck down) found that the majority of people who lost coverage were already working; they simply could not navigate the documentation system.

Access Note

The populations most likely to lose Medicaid under OBBBA work requirements are: workers in informal or gig economies (no easy documentation), people with episodic employment (construction, agriculture, service industry), caregivers of children and elderly family members, and people with mental health or substance use conditions that affect documentation capacity. These populations overlap significantly with women of reproductive age and are disproportionately Black and Latina. The CBO's 4.6–5.3 million figure is a floor, not a ceiling.

In Your District

Medicaid is the primary payer for 42% of all births in the United States. In states with high Medicaid birth rates (Louisiana: 72%, Mississippi: 65%, Arkansas: 60%), the OBBBA Medicaid purge is a direct maternal health intervention. Women who lose Medicaid mid-pregnancy lose prenatal care. Women who lose Medicaid in their postpartum period lose the follow-up care where postpartum depression, hemorrhage complications, and hypertension are most commonly detected. The 4.6–5.3 million coverage losses are not distributed randomly. They are concentrated in the districts with the highest maternal mortality rates in the country.

The Cost of Restriction

$133 Billion: What Abortion Restriction Costs the Economy Every Year

The Institute for Women's Policy Research estimates that abortion restrictions cost the US economy $133 billion annually in lost wages, reduced workforce participation, and increased healthcare spending. This figure has been peer-reviewed, replicated, and updated. It is not advocacy math. It is economic accounting.

The mechanism is straightforward: forced births suppress workforce participation. The IFPR estimates that 556,000 women are removed from the workforce annually due to abortion restrictions — through forced childbearing, caregiving obligations, and the career interruptions that accompany unplanned pregnancy. At median female wages, that is the $133 billion figure. The number does not include the downstream costs to children born into economic instability, the increased public assistance costs, or the long-term productivity drag on the economy.

🔬 Clinical Gem

The Turnaway Study — the most rigorous longitudinal study of the economic effects of abortion denial — found that women denied abortions were three times more likely to be unemployed six months later, more likely to live in poverty four years later, and more likely to remain in contact with abusive partners. These are not moral arguments. They are clinical and economic outcomes, tracked over time, with a control group. The data is unambiguous. Abortion denial is an economic intervention with predictable, measurable effects on women's financial trajectory.

In Your District

556,000 women removed from the workforce annually is not distributed equally across the country. It is concentrated in the 13 states with near-total abortion bans — states that are also, without exception, among the states with the lowest female labor force participation rates, the lowest female median wages, and the highest rates of women living below the poverty line. The economic suppression is not a coincidence of geography. It is the predictable outcome of the policy. Any campaign operating in a ban state can put specific numbers to this in their district with IFPR's state-level data.

Maternity Costs & Brain Drain

The Most Expensive Place to Have a Baby. The Fastest Exodus of Prime-Age Workers.

The United States is the most expensive country in the developed world in which to give birth. The average uncomplicated vaginal delivery costs $14,000. A cesarean section averages $26,000. Even with insurance, out-of-pocket costs for a normal delivery average $2,854 — and rising. The $32 billion annual cost of maternal morbidity — preventable complications, extended hospital stays, long-term health consequences — is carried primarily by Medicaid, which is simultaneously being cut.

The brain drain from ban states is quantifiable and accelerating. An analysis by Moving.com and the National Association of Realtors found that approximately 129,000 residents left the 13 states with near-total abortion bans in the 12 months following Dobbs. These are not retirees. They are prime-age, college-educated workers — the demographic with the highest workforce contribution and the highest taxpayer value. They are not moving because of the weather.

The Gap

Medical debt was removed from credit reporting by the CFPB rule finalized in 2024. The Trump administration reversed that rule, putting medical debt back on credit reports effective 2025. The direct effect on reproductive healthcare: the women who incur medical debt from unplanned deliveries, complications, and the downstream healthcare costs of forced birth now have that debt on their credit reports — affecting their ability to rent housing, access car loans, and obtain employment. The financial consequences of a forced birth now extend to the credit system.

In Your District

The abortion travel tax is $1,250 on average — the cost of transportation, lodging, childcare, and lost wages for a woman who must cross state lines to access abortion care. For a woman earning minimum wage in Mississippi ($7.25/hour), $1,250 is 17 weeks of food budget. For a single mother with two children in Louisiana, it is the cost that makes abortion access practically impossible even if legally available in a neighboring state. The travel tax is not paid in equal amounts. It is paid in full by women without resources and paid nothing by women with them.

Forward Look — Next 90 Days

The Economic Deadlines Coming Faster Than Anyone Is Acknowledging

⚡ Watch Immediately

EPTC Expiration — January 1, 2026: Open enrollment closes December 15, 2025. Patients on ACA marketplace plans need to know their January premium now. Healthcare providers in high-marketplace-enrollment states should prepare for a coverage disruption wave beginning in Q1. The premium cliff is not theoretical — it is a billing event with a specific date.

⚡ Watch Immediately

OBBBA Section 71113 Implementation Timeline: Medicaid work requirements take effect on the schedule set by each state's implementation plan. Watch your state's Medicaid agency. Some states will move fast. The first coverage terminations for failure to document work requirements could begin as early as Q2 2026 in fast-moving states. Every clinical practice whose patient panel is more than 30% Medicaid should be modeling this now.

Action Required

WIC Funding Cliff Monitoring: WIC (the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children) is operating on a continuing resolution that expires. WIC serves 6.7 million low-income pregnant women, new mothers, infants, and children up to age 5. A WIC funding lapse is a direct maternal and infant nutrition intervention with immediate clinical consequences. Know your state's WIC funding status and flag it for every obstetric patient whose family income qualifies.

Plan For

Medical Debt on Credit Reports — 2025 Forward: The CFPB rule removing medical debt from credit reports was reversed. Medical debt is back on credit reports. Counsel patients on healthcare billing advocacy resources, charity care applications, and the specific credit reporting timelines for medical debt. The financial consequences of healthcare now extend beyond the bill — they extend to credit, housing, and employment.

The Frame Going Forward

The Cost Is Not Incidental. The Cost Is the Mechanism.

The $133 billion annual economic drag from abortion restrictions is not a side effect of ideological policy. It is the mechanism by which the restriction operates. When a woman cannot afford to travel for an abortion, the policy achieved its goal through economics rather than enforcement. When a woman loses Medicaid coverage mid-pregnancy because she cannot navigate documentation requirements, the policy achieved its goal through bureaucratic design rather than legislation.

The most powerful way to communicate reproductive restriction to constituents who have not experienced it directly is through the economic translation: their insurance bill, their workforce participation rate, their neighbor who left the state. The data exists. The state-level breakdowns exist. The Turnaway Study exists. The CBO scoring exists. What has been missing is the translation into the language of constituent impact — which is exactly what this domain produces.

Every dollar in this report represents a person who made a calculation the system made for them. That calculation has a zip code, a median wage, an insurance enrollment status. When campaigns understand that, they are no longer fighting abstract policy. They are fighting the bill that arrives in January.
