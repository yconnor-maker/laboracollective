The Guidelines Said One Thing.
Your Patients Needed Another.

When evidence shifts faster than protocols, the gap between what the literature says and what happens in the room is where outcomes are decided — and where practitioners have always been on their own.

Special Report  ·  Mid-2025 through Early 2026  ·  Labora Rounds

The Signal

The Signal: A landmark RCT on betamethasone in late preterm twins upends a widely applied protocol — while simultaneously, federal cuts are eliminating the infrastructure that translates new evidence into updated guidelines.

The Contradiction: We now have better evidence about what works. We are simultaneously dismantling the systems that communicate it.

What This Means: Practitioners are being asked to practice at a higher standard with less support, less time, and a guidelines apparatus that is falling further behind what the science actually shows.

The Clinical Landscape

The Evidence Is Moving. The Infrastructure Isn't.

For a decade, the late preterm singleton betamethasone trial was the anchor finding — antenatal corticosteroids at 34–36 weeks reduced respiratory morbidity, and the field moved accordingly. That logic extended, in practice, to twins. Same gestational window, similar lung maturation biology, same intervention. Except the evidence for that extension was extrapolated, not generated.

A 2025 multicenter RCT changed that. In late preterm twin gestations, betamethasone did not replicate the singleton benefit — and in some subgroups showed trends toward harm that require careful monitoring. The finding does not invalidate the singleton data. It does require that practitioners who have been applying the protocol reflexively to every late preterm presentation stop and reassess in real time.

This is not a guidelines failure. Guidelines work on 18-to-36-month revision cycles. This is a clinical intelligence gap — the interval between when the evidence becomes clear and when the updated protocol reaches the exam room. That interval has always existed. What has changed is who bears the risk of that gap. In a system where institutional support for continuing education is being cut, where the federal infrastructure that funds guideline development is being dismantled, and where the time between patient encounters has compressed to minutes, that gap now falls entirely on the individual practitioner.

Clinical Gem

The late preterm twin betamethasone finding: The 2025 RCT (Gyamfi-Bannerman et al. multicenter) evaluated antenatal betamethasone in 34 0/7–36 6/7 week twin gestations and found no significant reduction in the primary composite respiratory outcome compared to placebo. A secondary subgroup analysis noted higher rates of neonatal hypoglycemia in the treatment arm. The finding applies to dichorionic-diamniotic and monochorionic-diamniotic twins specifically — data on monoamniotic twins remains limited.

Protocol update: SMFM released a practice statement acknowledging the trial; ACOG has not yet issued a full guideline update. Practitioners should document individualized risk assessment for each late preterm twin presentation rather than applying the singleton protocol by default.

Federal & Guideline Context

The Infrastructure That Turns Evidence Into Practice Is Being Dismantled

The betamethasone finding is one data point. The structural context is the story. The agencies and funding streams that translate RCT findings into clinical guidelines — AHRQ, NICHD, the SMFM Research Committee, the NIH Maternal-Fetal Medicine Units Network itself — have all faced significant disruption in the mid-2025 through early 2026 period.

The MFMU Network, which produced some of the most consequential obstetric RCTs of the last 30 years — including the original late preterm singleton betamethasone trial — received contract modification notices in late 2025 as part of broader NIH restructuring. The practical effect: fewer sites, longer enrollment timelines, slower answers. The obstetric questions that require multi-site trials to answer at statistical power are now harder to answer.

AHRQ's comparative effectiveness research program, which produces the systematic reviews that underpin ACOG guideline updates, had its budget reduced in the FY2026 continuing resolution. The agency that tells us what the evidence says is being asked to do that work with fewer resources, which means longer intervals between guideline updates, which means the gap between what the literature shows and what the protocol says grows wider.

Practice Change

Document your reasoning, now: As guidelines lag behind evidence, the clinical record is your protection. For late preterm presentations, document the specific trial data you reviewed, the individualized patient factors you weighted, and the shared decision-making conversation you had. "Per protocol" is no longer a complete answer when the protocol is under active revision.

Clinical Practice Intelligence

What Is Actually Changing in the Exam Room

The betamethasone finding is the headline, but it represents a broader pattern. Multiple clinical protocols that practitioners have used reflexively are under active evidence review in this period, and the political disruption of guideline infrastructure means that evidence and practice are diverging in ways that were previously self-correcting.

Access Note

Who bears the evidence gap: Practitioners in safety-net settings and rural hospitals are least likely to have access to grand rounds presentations, journal clubs, or CME on rapidly evolving evidence. The betamethasone finding reached academic MFM programs quickly. How long does it take to reach the community OB in rural Alabama who sees three twin pregnancies a year? That gap is a health equity problem, not just a knowledge translation problem.

The Gap

Destroyed data infrastructure: The NIH Women's Health Initiative program offices that maintained longitudinal clinical datasets were restructured in 2025. Some datasets that were actively informing guideline development — particularly around hormonal therapy in peri- and postmenopausal women — are now inaccessible pending reorganization. The field does not know what it has lost yet.

State Divergence

Two Clinical Environments, One Country

States Investing in Clinical Standards

  • California — Mandated Perinatal Quality Collaborative participation for all Level II+ birth hospitals; PQC incorporates new evidence into statewide protocols within 90 days of major trial publication
  • Massachusetts — State CME requirement updated to include annual obstetric protocol review; funded through state medical board, not federal grants
  • Washington — UW/Harborview MFM Network serving rural hospitals includes standing evidence update service; betamethasone guidance distributed within 30 days of SMFM statement
  • New York — ACOG District II operating independent guideline translation program after federal cuts; covers 1,400+ obstetricians statewide

States With Widening Evidence Gap

  • Texas — No statewide PQC participation requirement; rural practitioners with no academic MFM access are practicing on 18-month-old protocols with no update mechanism
  • Mississippi — Single Level III NICU covers entire state; network bandwidth insufficient to distribute rapid evidence updates to community providers
  • Idaho — Post-physician exodus following abortion criminalization, remaining OBs carrying caseloads that leave zero time for protocol review or CME
  • Louisiana — Medicaid MFM consultation network defunded in FY2026 state budget; community OBs now practicing high-risk obstetrics without specialist access
In Your District

The caseload math: A community OB in a ban state covering a rural catchment of 40,000 people is seeing between 400 and 600 deliveries annually. If 2–3% involve late preterm twin gestations, that's 8–18 cases per year where the betamethasone decision must be made — with no MFM on staff, no grand rounds, and no CME budget. The evidence exists. The system that communicates it to that practitioner does not.

In states where physician workforce has collapsed under criminalization pressure, the practitioners still there are managing complexity their predecessors referred. They are doing it with guidelines that are 18 months behind the literature and no support structure to close that gap.

Clinical Reality

What Happens in the Room

The 2025 twin betamethasone trial doesn't tell practitioners to never use corticosteroids in late preterm twins. It tells them to stop using the singleton evidence as the justification for twin practice. That distinction matters, and it is exactly the kind of distinction that gets lost when the guideline just says "betamethasone at 34–36 weeks" with no subspecification.

Shared decision-making is now essential and is now harder. The practitioner explaining to a patient at 35 weeks with twins why they are not recommending the steroid course that the patient's friend received — and may have expected — is doing clinical education in a charged, emotional moment without a guideline to point to. That conversation requires time and confidence in the evidence that the current system does not support well.

For the practitioner: the SMFM statement, published in response to the 2025 trial, is the current clinical anchor. It is not a full guideline but it is the best available synthesis, it is SMFM-credentialed, and it can be cited in documentation. Use it. Do not wait for the ACOG update that may not come before your next late preterm twin presentation.

Clinical Gem

Referral threshold for late preterm twins: Given the protocol uncertainty, any late preterm twin presentation at 34–36 weeks should trigger MFM consultation if available — not because the community OB cannot manage the delivery, but because the individualized betamethasone risk-benefit conversation benefits from subspecialty documentation. In facilities without MFM access, SMFM has a teleconsult program; document the attempt regardless of outcome.

Critical Alert

Political vs. evidence-based guideline changes: In the same period as the twin betamethasone update, mifepristone clinical guidance was modified under political rather than clinical pressure. Practitioners must now actively distinguish between guideline revisions driven by new evidence (apply them) and revisions driven by regulatory or political interference (evaluate them critically, document your independent clinical reasoning). These are not the same thing and conflating them is a patient safety risk.

Practice Change

Build your own evidence update system: Given federal infrastructure disruption, practitioners cannot rely on guidelines to stay current. Minimum viable system: SMFM Practice Bulletin email alerts (free, subscribe at smfm.org), NEJM Evidence app (paid but CME-eligible), one peer-reviewed Twitter/X list of MFMs posting trial commentary. This is not adequate. It is what we have.

Forward Look

What Happens Next, and When

The evidence on late preterm twin corticosteroids will not resolve quickly. ACOG's guideline update cycle means a formal recommendation is most likely mid-2026. The MFMU Network disruption means the follow-up questions — optimal dosing, specific subgroup benefit — cannot be answered at the speed the field needs. Practitioners are carrying this uncertainty for the foreseeable future.

The broader clinical practice intelligence picture is one of accelerating divergence: states with functioning clinical quality infrastructure will absorb new evidence in weeks; states without it will absorb it in years, if at all. That divergence is a health outcome story, not just an administrative one.

Recommended Actions

What to Do with This

For All Clinicians

  • Subscribe to SMFM Practice Bulletin email alerts — this is now the fastest path to evidence-based protocol updates when ACOG is delayed
  • Update your late preterm twin betamethasone consent and documentation workflow to reflect the 2025 trial; "per standard protocol" is no longer adequate documentation
  • Audit your current protocol set for guidelines older than 24 months — in the current environment, those are most likely to be outdated
  • Build a personal evidence alert system; the institutional infrastructure for this is no longer reliable

For Clinical Leaders and Department Chiefs

  • Convene a rapid protocol review specifically for late preterm twin management — include MFM, neonatology, and nursing; document the institutional decision and disseminate
  • Create an institutional mechanism to distinguish between politically-driven guideline changes (mifepristone) and evidence-driven ones (betamethasone twins) — practitioners need that guidance from leadership, not their own read of the political landscape
  • Advocate with your hospital system for CME budget restoration — the practitioners most at risk for evidence lag are those with the least CME access, and that is now a quality and liability issue
  • Connect with your state perinatal quality collaborative; if your state doesn't have one, that is the advocacy project

For Advocates and Policy Staff

  • Restore AHRQ systematic review capacity — this is the mechanism that turns primary literature into clinical guidance, and it is being cut without public understanding of what is being lost
  • Defend MFMU Network funding — the obstetric RCTs that answered the betamethasone questions in the first place came from this network; without it, major clinical questions remain unanswered for a generation
  • The clinical practice intelligence gap is a health equity story — communities served by under-resourced practitioners are the last to receive updated evidence and the first to experience the harm of outdated protocols
