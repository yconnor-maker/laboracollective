Opening

The Legal Architecture of Women's Bodily Autonomy Is Being Dismantled from Multiple Directions at Once

A lawyer in Missouri who has a miscarriage is charged with a crime. A physician in Idaho waits for a patient's condition to become imminently life-threatening before intervening — because the law gives her no other choice and the criminal exposure is real. A reproductive health database in Texas issues subpoenas for medical records across state lines. These are not worst-case scenarios. They are documented events in the period covered by this report.

Understanding the current legal landscape requires holding two things at once: first, the tools being deployed are a mix of old (Comstock Act, pre-Roe statutes), resurrected (feticide laws, privacy vacatur), and newly constructed (OBBBA Medicaid provisions, Title X administrative decapitation); second, the institutional mechanisms supposed to check this — the courts, HHS enforcement, the DOJ — have been captured, hollowed out, or deliberately redirected.

What follows is a systematic account of where things stand, what changed in this period, and what the next 90 days require.

Federal Legal Architecture

HIPAA Vacated. Comstock Revived. The Federal Floor Is Gone.

The most consequential federal legal development of this period is Purl v. HHS. A federal district court in Texas vacated the HHS rule extending HIPAA protections to reproductive health information — specifically the provision preventing covered entities from disclosing reproductive health records to state authorities investigating out-of-state abortions. The administration chose not to appeal. The rule is gone.

What this means in practice: a patient in Texas who traveled to New Mexico for a legal abortion no longer has federal protection against her provider being compelled to produce those records in response to a state subpoena. State law now governs — which means patients in ban states are governed by ban-state law even when they access care elsewhere.

⚠ Critical Alert

Purl v. HHS is in effect now. If you practice in a shield law state (New York, California, Massachusetts, Colorado, Illinois), your state protection still applies — but you must document your data privacy policy explicitly. If you are in a state without a shield law, you have no federal floor. Know what your EHR system does when served with an out-of-state subpoena. If you do not know, find out this week.

The second major development is the formal resurrection of the Comstock Act as an active legal tool. The DOJ issued a memo asserting Comstock Act applicability to mifepristone and misoprostol by mail, effectively threatening to ban medication abortion through the postal service without new legislation. The memo has not been enforced yet. Its existence is the threat — and every telehealth medication abortion provider is making legal decisions in its shadow.

Practice Change

If your practice uses mail-order pharmacy or telehealth medication abortion, your legal exposure changed with the Comstock memo. State shield laws in protective states offer provider protection — but they do not protect the federal prosecution of the pharmacy or the mail carrier. Follow evolving guidance from ACOG, your state medical association, and your malpractice carrier. Do not operate on 2023 legal frameworks. They are outdated.

In Your District

If the Comstock Act is enforced against medication abortion by mail, the estimated 20+ states protecting abortion access through telehealth lose that access pathway immediately — without a single legislative vote. In rural districts where the nearest in-person provider is 100+ miles away, mail-order medication abortion is not a convenience. It is the only access point. Comstock enforcement eliminates it with no debate and no constituency to lobby in time to stop it.

Title X & Medicaid

The Administrative Kill Shot: Defunding Without Legislation

Title X — the federal family planning program — has been gutted by administrative action, not repeal. The Office of Population Affairs, the HHS division that administers Title X grants, has been reduced to a single officer. This is a deliberate strategy: eliminate the operational infrastructure so grants cannot be renewed, technical assistance cannot be provided, and the program atrophies from inside. The result is a projected 55% revenue loss for Title X grantees — clinics providing contraception, STI testing, cancer screening, and pregnancy testing to 4 million low-income patients annually.

Access Note

Title X patients are disproportionately uninsured, low-income, and rural. They are patients at community health centers, county health departments, rural clinics — providers who depend on Title X reimbursement to operate at all. When the OPA cannot function, those providers cut services or close. There is no private sector equivalent positioned to absorb 4 million low-income patients losing their only source of family planning care.

Medina v. Planned Parenthood South Atlantic is currently before the Supreme Court. The question: whether states can exclude qualified providers from Medicaid solely because they offer abortion. If the Court rules against the right to choose providers, every state can immediately cut abortion providers from Medicaid networks — regardless of what other services those providers offer.

In Your District

Planned Parenthood serves approximately 2.4 million Medicaid patients annually for services unrelated to abortion — contraception, STI testing, cancer screening, wellness exams. In states where Planned Parenthood is the only accessible Medicaid provider for miles, losing Medicaid reimbursement means clinic closure. The patients who lose access are not wealthy women with alternatives. They are the constituents carrying the highest burden of unmet healthcare need in every district where this plays out.

Criminal Prosecution

210 Charges. 31 for Miscarriage or Stillbirth. This Is the Enforcement Environment.

Since Dobbs, Pregnancy Justice has documented 210 criminal charges in connection with pregnancy outcomes. Thirty-one involve miscarriages or stillbirths — not elective abortions, but pregnancy losses. The population being prosecuted is predominantly low-income Black women in states with near-total bans, charged under fetal homicide statutes, feticide laws, and child endangerment provisions.

The prosecution mechanism requires no law specifically targeting abortion. It requires a creative prosecutor, a broad fetal personhood statute, and a patient who had a bad outcome — regardless of cause. In states where drug use during pregnancy triggers criminal charges, any substance in a toxicology screen becomes evidence. In states where fetal homicide laws extend to early pregnancy, a documented miscarriage can be the basis for a homicide investigation.

🔬 Clinical Gem

Miscarriage occurs in 10–20% of known pregnancies. Stillbirth affects approximately 1 in 160. Both are common, medically complex events with multiple causes — many impossible to distinguish from induced termination on clinical examination alone. In ban states, your documentation of an incomplete abortion presentation is potentially a legal document. Document the clinical presentation. Document what is not known. Document the differential. This is the current standard of care in the current legal environment.

The Gap

There is no comprehensive federal database of pregnancy-related prosecutions. The 210 number comes from Pregnancy Justice doing manual tracking — not the CDC, the DOJ, or any government entity. The administration eliminated CDC reproductive health data programs that would have surfaced this pattern systematically. The data about criminal prosecution of pregnancy loss is not being collected by the agencies responsible for collecting it.

Forward Look — Next 90 Days

The Decisions That Will Define the Decade

⚡ Watch Immediately

Medina v. Planned Parenthood — SCOTUS Decision (Spring 2026): The most consequential reproductive rights ruling since Dobbs. If states can exclude abortion providers from Medicaid, every Medicaid-dependent patient in a restrictive state loses their provider for all services. Watch the decision date. Legal actions begin within 24 hours of publication.

⚡ Watch Immediately

Comstock Act Enforcement Signal: The DOJ memo is a loaded threat. Watch for any administrative signal — changes in USPS guidance, any federal case against a pharmacy or mail service. The moment enforcement begins, every telehealth medication abortion service faces a federal criminal exposure question it cannot answer quickly enough.

Action Required

Update Patient Privacy Protocols Now: Purl v. HHS is in effect. If your practice does not have a documented policy on responding to out-of-state subpoenas for reproductive health records, you need one immediately. Your malpractice carrier's 2024 guidance may be insufficient. Seek updated state medical association guidance from after November 2025.

Action Required

Know Your Shield Law: If you practice in a shield law state, know exactly what it covers. Texas v. Bruck validated New York's architecture — but shield laws are not uniform. They differ on provider protection, patient protection, and services covered. Know your state's specific law before you need it under emergency conditions.

Plan For

IVF Legal Exposure — 2026: Fifteen states have active embryo personhood legislation creating direct legal exposure for IVF clinics. The Alabama 2024 frozen embryo ruling is the leading precedent. IVF providers in these states operate under conditions that could change with a single court decision. Informed consent for IVF patients now includes legal risk — to the clinic, to the embryos, and to the patient.

The Frame Going Forward

The Scaffold Is Being Built Faster Than It Can Be Dismantled

The legal architecture being constructed against reproductive rights has structural logic: each piece reinforces the next. HIPAA vacatur enables state surveillance. State surveillance enables prosecution. Prosecution enables criminalization norms. Changed clinical behavior harms patients. The harm becomes evidence that reproductive healthcare is dangerous — which justifies the next restriction.

Understanding this is not pessimism. It is the prerequisite for interrupting it. Texas v. Bruck showed that shield law architecture can hold. Missouri's Amendment 3 showed that constitutional protection can be secured by ballot initiative even in hostile legislative environments. The Pregnancy Justice prosecution tracker showed that naming and counting the harm matters — because data that doesn't exist can't be acted on.

The women being prosecuted for miscarriages are not abstractions. They came through someone's clinic, cared for by someone who didn't know that the documentation they created would become evidence in a criminal case. Knowing that is what changes practice. Know it.
