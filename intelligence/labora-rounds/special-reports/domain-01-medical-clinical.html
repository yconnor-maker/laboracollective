Breast Cancer

The Best Data We've Had in a Generation — and What's Being Done to Undermine It

The most significant clinical finding of this period came from the ESMO Congress: the DESTINY-Breast05 Phase 3 trial data. For patients with high-risk HER2-positive early-stage breast cancer who had residual invasive disease after neoadjuvant therapy, trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd, Enhertu) reduced the risk of disease recurrence or death by 53%.

To be precise: at three years, invasive disease-free survival reached 92.4% in the T-DXd group versus 83.7% for patients on T-DM1. That is not a marginal improvement. For HER2-positive breast cancer — more aggressive and historically more treatable than other subtypes — this is the kind of data that rewrites tumor board conversations immediately.

🔬 Clinical Gem

The improvement comes with a meaningful tradeoff. Interstitial lung disease occurred in 9.6% of patients on T-DXd versus 1.6% on T-DM1 — a sixfold increase. Integrating this drug as standard of care means establishing explicit pulmonology partnerships before you prescribe, not after a patient develops symptoms. The monitoring protocol requires pulmonary surveillance built in, not bolted on after the fact.

Access Note

Early discontinuation or non-adherence to endocrine therapy in breast cancer patients reaches 60%. These are life-saving medications patients stop taking — usually because of side effects managed poorly and support systems that aren't there. No clinical breakthrough survives inadequate patient navigation. Adherence infrastructure is as much a clinical tool as the drug itself.

In Your District

HER2-positive breast cancer disproportionately affects younger women and is more common in Black women than white women. In districts where Medicaid is the primary payer and specialty oncology access is limited, the 53% recurrence reduction from T-DXd will remain theoretical — not because the drug doesn't exist, but because the navigation infrastructure to get patients to it, keep them on it, and monitor pulmonary risk doesn't exist in most safety-net settings.

HRT & Menopause

A Reckoning 20 Years in the Making — and the Access Gap That Remains

In November 2025, the FDA initiated removal of the black box warning from hormone replacement therapy products. This was not a minor labeling update. It was a formal acknowledgment that the medical establishment got this catastrophically wrong for two decades — and that women paid for that error with their health, cognitive function, cardiovascular outcomes, and bones.

In 2002, the Women's Health Initiative published findings that were misinterpreted — widely, influentially, catastrophically — to mean that estrogen causes breast cancer and cardiovascular disease. HRT prescriptions collapsed. An entire generation of women was undertreated for vasomotor symptoms, bone density loss, cognitive changes, and cardiovascular risk. The original interpretation was wrong. The FDA's 2025 action corrects the record.

Practice Change

The FDA's black box removal changes your informed consent conversation, your prior authorization language, and your patient education materials — immediately. Document the FDA's own language characterizing estrogen as "a key hormone essential for optimal bodily function." You will need it when fighting prior authorization denials from insurers who haven't updated their medical policies to match the new regulatory reality.

⚠ Critical Alert

The FDA issued a Boxed Warning on Veozah (fezolinetant) after a post-marketing case of serious liver injury at 40 days of treatment. The new protocol is mandatory: baseline liver function tests, monthly LFTs for the first three months, then at months 6 and 9. Veozah cannot be initiated if baseline ALT/AST or total bilirubin are at or above twice the upper limit of normal. The women who most need non-hormonal options are often those who can't afford monthly lab copays.

In Your District

The first generic version of Premarin in over 30 years was approved in this period. In theory, this makes HRT affordable for millions of women priced out of branded estrogen therapy. In practice: watch insurance formularies in your district. Generic availability does not equal insurance coverage. In the first year, expect prior authorization friction — especially in states where Medicaid formulary updates lag FDA approval by 12–18 months.

Uterine Fibroids

The 7× Disparity That Is Not About Biology

Black women are 2 to 3 times more likely to develop uterine fibroids than white women and experience significantly more severe symptoms. They also receive invasive open abdominal hysterectomies at 7 times the rate of white women with the same diagnosis.

Seven times. Not because the disease is different. Not because minimally invasive options — uterine artery embolization, myomectomy — are clinically contraindicated. Because of who is in the room making the referral decision, and what assumptions are embedded in that moment. This is implicit bias operating as a clinical pathway. The outcome disparity is not a mystery.

Practice Change

The NIH's policy revision replacing "sex/gender" with strictly biological "sex" means federal researchers are now constrained from studying how implicit bias drives these disparities. Your practice cannot wait for federal data to correct what you can see in your own referral patterns. Audit your fibroid surgical pathway now. What percentage of your Black patients with fibroids are referred for open hysterectomy versus minimally invasive options? If the answer surprises you, it shouldn't — and it needs to change.

🔬 Clinical Gem

Boston Medical Center's "Centering Fibroids" model — shared group visits and peer support around fibroid management — has demonstrated meaningful improvements in symptom management and treatment satisfaction. New AI diagnostic tools are identifying fibroid burden earlier and with greater precision. These are not pilot programs. They are the standard of care that should be baseline, not exception.

Federal Research Infrastructure

The Architecture Being Dismantled While You're Not Looking

The clinical advances of this period exist against deliberate federal disinvestment in the research and data systems that make women's health progress possible. This is not the background. This is the central story.

The NIH terminated regional center contracts for the Women's Health Initiative — the landmark 30-year longitudinal study tracking chronic disease outcomes in post-menopausal women. This is not a budget line. It is the primary dataset for understanding how heart disease, cancer, cognitive decline, and bone loss develop in women over time. Once the longitudinal cohorts are disbanded, you cannot recreate 30 years of prospective data. What we lose is not just current findings — it is the ability to ask new questions of old cohorts. It is irreplaceable in the literal sense.

The Gap

The NIH's FY2026 budget proposal included a $17.97 billion cut — a 37% reduction — and proposed collapsing 27 institutes into 5. The National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities, which represents $534 million in annual research on communities with the worst health outcomes, was targeted for complete elimination. The framing was "anti-DEI." The effect is defunding the only federal research infrastructure dedicated to understanding why Black women die in childbirth at three times the rate of white women.

In Your District

The government shutdown in October forced NIH into contingency operations with approximately 24% of staff for over two weeks. Peer review panels froze. Clinical trials paused. Grant success rates dropped further. Every district in this country has researchers, clinicians, and patients whose work depends on NIH funding. The cuts are not abstract. They are the clinical trial that didn't open, the grant that wasn't reviewed, the researcher who left for a country that still funds science.

The Frame Going Forward

The Science Moved. The System Is Moving Backward.

What the last six months reveal is a clinical landscape defined by a specific contradiction: extraordinary scientific capability existing alongside deliberate structural sabotage. The drugs work. The models work. The data is clear. And the federal apparatus that should be funding, protecting, and expanding access to that evidence is instead being weaponized against it.

The DESTINY-Breast05 data will save lives — in institutions that have pulmonology partnerships and adherence infrastructure. The HRT correction will reverse 20 years of harm — for patients whose providers have been re-educated and whose insurers have updated formularies. The fibroid disparity will persist — until the referral pathway is audited and changed in every practice where it exists.

The ceiling has never been higher. The floor has never needed more defending. Both of those things are true at the same time. Your job is to work at both levels simultaneously.
