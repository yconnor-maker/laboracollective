The Stories

Story 01 — GYN

The Black Box Warning on Hormone Therapy Is Gone. Here’s What That Actually Means.

In November 2025, the FDA removed the black box warning from menopausal hormone therapy that had been in place since 2002. Updated labeling arrived in January 2026. For 23 years, the most prominent warning category in FDA labeling sat on medications that, for the majority of women who needed them, were not as dangerous as that warning implied.

The warning came from the Women’s Health Initiative trial, which was biased toward women in their 60s and 70s, when most women start hormone therapy in their 40s and 50s. When later studies used current formulations in appropriately aged populations, the same risks didn’t replicate. The result was 23 years of undertreated suffering — approximately 1.3 million women annually experiencing moderate-to-severe symptoms that significantly impair sleep, work, and relationships.

What’s changed is that the starting point of the conversation is now accurate science, not 23-year-old fear. Women under 60, or within 10 years of menopause onset, have a substantially different risk profile than the study population. Contraindications still exist. For women who prefer non-hormonal options, fezolinetant (Veozah) reduces vasomotor symptoms 50–65% without hormones.

What this means for you

If you have been suffering through menopause symptoms and were told hormone therapy wasn’t safe for you — ask again. Bring this to your provider. Ask them to walk through your specific risk profile with the updated guidance. You deserve a conversation, not a categorical no based on outdated data.

Story 02 — GYN

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act Was Supposed to Protect You. They’re Coming for It.

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, effective June 2023, requires employers with 15 or more employees to provide reasonable accommodations for pregnancy and pregnancy-related conditions. The EEOC’s current regulations interpret “pregnancy-related conditions” to include infertility treatment, menstruation, menopause, and contraception.

That interpretation is now under threat. EEOC Chair Andrea Lucas has publicly signaled plans to narrow the definition — potentially excluding fertility treatment, menstrual conditions, menopause, and contraception from protection. On January 29, 2026, the Commission voted 2-1 along party lines to centralize litigation control with the Republican Chair, giving her authority to move those revisions forward quickly.

If the definition narrows, workers lose federal protection for all of it. No required accommodation for fertility treatment. No required accommodation for menstrual conditions that affect work capacity. The ability to keep a job while managing reproductive health conditions determines whether patients can afford healthcare, maintain insurance, and sustain the financial stability that is itself a health determinant.

What this means for you

If you need a workplace accommodation for any reproductive health condition — fertility treatment, endometriosis, menopause, contraception — request it now and document the request. Current protections apply today. Regulatory changes take time. Use the window.

Story 03 — Reproductive Justice

The Louisiana Mifepristone Case Is Not a Louisiana Issue.

On February 24, 2026, a federal court in Louisiana heard arguments on a preliminary injunction that would end mail-order mifepristone access — not just in Louisiana, but nationwide. Every patient in every state, including California and New York, would lose the ability to receive this medication by mail. The hearing has happened. We are waiting for a ruling.

What makes this especially significant: on January 27, the Department of Justice filed a motion in this case that declined to defend the merits of the 2023 FDA decision. DOJ criticized the prior FDA risk assessment as possibly inadequate — essentially agreeing with Louisiana’s critique — while FDA indicated its own review would take approximately one year. The federal government is not defending an FDA approval it technically still maintains.

Mail-order medication abortion was the primary access innovation after Dobbs. A preliminary injunction ends that immediately — while legal challenges proceed, which could take years. The patients most harmed are the ones with the least capacity to absorb the loss: rural patients, patients without transportation, patients who work jobs without flexible scheduling.

What this means for you

Know which brick-and-mortar pharmacies in your area stock mifepristone. Know what in-person access looks like for your patients if mail-order ends overnight. If you use telemedicine medication abortion, have a contingency plan for your patients now.

Story 04 — Reproductive Justice

Your Pharmacist Can Now Refuse. And There Is No Federal Agency Left to Call.

On January 27, 2026, HHS Office for Civil Rights formally rescinded the guidance that had reminded 60,000 retail pharmacies of their civil rights obligations when dispensing contraceptives and medications used in reproductive care — including misoprostol, methotrexate, and mifepristone. Federal civil rights enforcement on pharmacy refusals is gone.

The medications covered are not limited to abortion. Misoprostol is used for miscarriage management, postpartum hemorrhage prevention, and cervical ripening. Methotrexate is used for ectopic pregnancy management, rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis, and cancer treatment. A patient with rheumatoid arthritis being refused her methotrexate by a pharmacist with a religious objection now has no federal recourse.

The same week this guidance was rescinded, HHS issued a Notice of Violation to Illinois for tying pharmacy non-discrimination requirements to conscience protections — threatening the state’s federal funding for trying to protect patients at the state level. The federal government is not just stepping back from enforcement. It is actively pressuring states that try to fill the gap.

What this means for you

Find out which pharmacies near you will reliably fill reproductive health prescriptions. Ask your provider to tell you specifically which pharmacy to go to. If you’re refused, document it: pharmacy name, medication, date, reason given. State protections may still apply and vary by location.
