The PWFA Was Supposed to Cover Your Whole Reproductive Life. It May Not For Long.

Dr. Yamicia Connor, MD, PhD, MPH  ·  March 11, 2026  ·  The Labora Signal

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act, effective June 2023, requires employers with 15 or more employees to provide reasonable accommodations for pregnancy and pregnancy-related conditions — unless doing so creates undue hardship. The EEOC's current regulations interpret "pregnancy-related conditions" to include infertility treatment, menstruation, menopause, and contraception.

That interpretation is now under threat. EEOC Chair Andrea Lucas has publicly signaled plans to narrow the definition — potentially excluding fertility treatment, menstrual conditions, menopause, and contraception from protection. On January 29, 2026, the Commission voted 2-1 along party lines to centralize litigation control with the Republican Chair, giving her authority to move those revisions forward quickly.

Most people think of the PWFA as protecting pregnant people. That's true, but it's only part of the story. The broad interpretation is what makes the law reach across a woman's entire reproductive life: time off for an IVF retrieval, schedule adjustments for endometriosis, temperature accommodations for severe hot flashes, insurance access for contraception. These are not edge cases. These are the daily places where reproductive health and economic survival intersect.

If the definition narrows, workers lose federal protection for all of it. No required accommodation for fertility treatment. No required accommodation for menstrual conditions that affect work capacity. No required coverage for contraception access. The employer gets to say no without legal consequence.

For your patients who work — and most of them do — this is directly clinical. The ability to keep a job while managing reproductive health conditions determines whether they can afford healthcare, maintain insurance, and sustain the financial stability that is itself a health determinant. The PWFA's broad interpretation was a recognition that reproductive health doesn't happen only during pregnancy, and that the workplace consequences of managing it reach across decades of a woman's life.

The narrowing of the definition doesn't require a law change. It requires only a regulatory revision — a process that, with centralized litigation authority, can move faster than most people realize. This is the particular vulnerability of rights built on regulatory interpretation rather than statutory text: they can be revised administratively without returning to Congress.

What this means for you

If you need a workplace accommodation for any reproductive health condition — fertility treatment, endometriosis, menopause, contraception — request it now and document the request. Current protections apply today. Regulatory changes take time, but the process has started. Use the window you have.
