How to Request and Document Accommodations Under Current Law

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act covers more than pregnancy — use this window while it remains intact

Published March 11, 2026 · GYN/RJ Week 1 Resource · The Labora Collective
⚠ Current Law Window — Act Now

On January 29, 2026, EEOC Chair Andrea Lucas voted to narrow the PWFA's interpretation, moving toward removing coverage of abortion, IVF, and fertility treatments from the federal accommodation framework. The law has not changed yet — but the enforcement posture has shifted. Use what you have while it is still available to you.

Why This Pack Exists

The Pregnant Workers Fairness Act (PWFA), signed June 2023, was the most significant expansion of workplace rights for pregnant and reproductive-health patients in decades. It covered not just pregnancy and childbirth — but miscarriage, stillbirth, infertility treatment, lactation, and contraception-related conditions. The EEOC's January 2026 definitional narrowing threatens to strip that coverage. This pack helps you document, request, and protect your accommodations under the law as it currently exists.

⚠ The Threat — What the Lucas Vote Does

The January 29 vote does not change the PWFA statute itself. It signals that EEOC will no longer actively enforce the 2024 final rule's expansive definitions, and may formally narrow them through rulemaking. That means: employers in at-will states may begin denying accommodations for IVF, abortion recovery, and fertility treatments, knowing EEOC is unlikely to pursue complaints. Your window to use these protections — especially for IVF — is now.

Accommodation Types

What You Can Request Under the PWFA

The PWFA's key distinction from the ADA: your condition does not have to qualify as a disability. It only needs to be a physical or mental condition related to, affected by, or arising out of pregnancy, childbirth, or related medical conditions. Employers must provide accommodations unless doing so creates an "undue hardship."

Request Protocol

How to Request an Accommodation: Step by Step

  1. 1
    Notify your employer in writing — immediately

    The PWFA does not require a specific form. You simply need to inform your employer that you have a physical condition related to pregnancy, childbirth, or a related medical condition and that you need an adjustment. Do this in writing via email — not verbally — so there is a timestamped record.

  2. 2
    Use clear language: invoke the PWFA explicitly

    You are not required to say "I'm requesting an accommodation under the PWFA" — but doing so triggers the employer's legal obligation to engage in the interactive process. Vague requests without a legal anchor can be ignored.

  3. 3
    Do not disclose more medical information than necessary

    The PWFA requires your employer to engage with your request — it does not require you to provide your full medical history. You may say "I have a pregnancy-related medical condition that requires schedule flexibility" without specifying IVF, miscarriage, or abortion.

  4. 4
    Obtain supporting documentation from your provider

    While not always required, provider documentation strengthens your request significantly. Ask your OB/GYN, reproductive endocrinologist, or primary care provider for a letter stating the functional limitations and recommended accommodations — without disclosing diagnosis details if you prefer.

    • Include: functional limitation (e.g., "cannot lift more than 10 lbs")
    • Include: estimated duration (e.g., "through end of IVF cycle, approx. 6–8 weeks")
    • Avoid: specific diagnoses if you prefer not to disclose
  5. 5
    Engage in the interactive process — document every conversation

    Your employer is legally required to engage in an "interactive process" — a good-faith dialogue about what accommodation is feasible. Follow up every conversation in writing: "Per our meeting today, my understanding is…" If your employer refuses to engage, that refusal itself is a violation.

  6. 6
    If denied: request the denial in writing, then escalate

    Ask for the specific reason for denial in writing. Your employer must demonstrate that the accommodation poses "undue hardship" — a high bar. If denied, file an EEOC charge. File promptly: you have 180 days from the discriminatory act (300 days in states with their own anti-discrimination agencies).

Sample Request Language

"I am writing to formally request a workplace accommodation under the Pregnant Workers Fairness Act. I have a physical condition related to [pregnancy / childbirth / a pregnancy-related medical condition] that limits my ability to [specific function — e.g., maintain a fixed 9-to-5 schedule]. I am requesting [specific accommodation — e.g., flexible start time between 9–10 AM on days I have medical appointments]. I am happy to discuss alternatives that would meet my medical needs. Please let me know if you need supporting documentation from my healthcare provider."

Documentation Protocol

What to Document and How to Keep It

PWFA claims hinge on documentation. The window for enforcement may narrow — build your record now, whether or not you currently anticipate needing it.

Critical Practice: Personal Email Archive

Always forward PWFA-related correspondence to a personal email account (Gmail, iCloud, etc.) immediately — not just your work email. If your employment ends, your access to work email ends with it. Your documentation will be inaccessible at the exact moment you need it most for a claim.

If You Are Denied

Escalation Pathway After Denial

  • Request the denial reason in writing. Do not accept a verbal denial without following up: "To confirm our conversation, I understand you are denying my accommodation request. Could you please provide the specific reason in writing?" This creates the record.
  • Determine whether your state has stronger protections. Several states — California, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Colorado — have their own pregnant worker accommodation laws that may be stronger than the PWFA and are not subject to EEOC enforcement shifts. Your state law may be your better path.
  • File an EEOC Charge promptly. The filing deadline is 180 days from the discriminatory act (300 days in FEPA states). Do not wait. File online at publicportal.eeoc.gov. The charge preserves your right to sue even if EEOC enforcement is slow or inconsistent under the current posture.
  • Contact a plaintiff-side employment attorney. Many employment attorneys who handle pregnancy discrimination cases work on contingency. National Women's Law Center (nwlc.org) and A Better Balance (abetterbalance.org) can refer to attorneys in your state. The PWFA has robust attorney's fees provisions.
  • Consider whether retaliation is occurring. If you experienced any adverse employment action after making your PWFA request — demotion, reduced hours, negative performance review, termination — that retaliation is independently actionable under the PWFA and Title VII. Document it immediately.
State Law Alternatives

States With Stronger Protections Than the PWFA

The federal PWFA sets a floor. Several states have enacted laws that go further — covering smaller employers, requiring longer accommodation periods, or explicitly including conditions the EEOC is now seeking to exclude. In these states, pursue your state claim in parallel with or instead of the federal claim.
