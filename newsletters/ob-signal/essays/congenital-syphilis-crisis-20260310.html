OB Essay  ·  Syphilis & Public Health Infrastructure

Why We Don't Know What We're Doing — And Who's Responsible

Dr. Yamicia Connor, MD, PhD, MPH  ·  March 10, 2026  ·  The Labora Signal

Congenital syphilis cases in the U.S. hit 3,882 in 2022 — a 937% increase since 2012. These are babies born with a disease their mothers transmitted during pregnancy. A disease we have been able to treat since 1943. A disease that is entirely preventable if we catch it in time.

The treatment is Bicillin L-A — penicillin G benzathine — the only antibiotic proven to cross the placenta and protect the fetus. It is currently on FDA shortage. The alternatives have documented treatment failures in pregnancy. So we have a surge, a medication shortage, and a clinical workforce that doesn't see syphilis often enough to have the pattern locked in. This is a system in trouble.

Medicine is a pattern recognition sport. You see something enough times that a part of your brain takes over — the part that doesn't require executive thinking. That's efficient and it saves lives, but only for the things we see often. Gonorrhea, chlamydia, BV — those we treat in our sleep. Syphilis, until recently, most of us rarely saw. When you don't see something often, you don't just forget the treatment — you forget to recognize it at all. That's where cases get missed.

This is not a failure of individual clinicians. It is a predictable consequence of an infection becoming rare enough that a generation of providers never built the pattern, followed by that infection coming back fast enough that those same providers are now the ones in the room with it. The solution isn't blame. The solution is structural support — surveillance that catches surges early, education that refreshes the pattern, and treatment that is actually available when you need it.

Part of what is driving this is the collapse of public health infrastructure over decades — not overnight, but steadily. STI screening programs were defunded. The surveillance systems that should have flagged the surge earlier have been weakened. The CDC's Division of Reproductive Health — the body whose job it was to go down these rabbit holes, connect the dots between what one physician in Louisiana is seeing and what another physician in Minnesota is seeing, and mobilize a response — has had two of its three branches eliminated. When a physician sees something unusual today, there is no longer a well-resourced federal body to call. The observation goes nowhere.

The gap between what one clinician sees in their practice and what a national surveillance system can detect is exactly where preventable deaths hide. A physician in a rural county who suddenly sees three cases of secondary syphilis in a month knows something is happening. Without functioning surveillance infrastructure, that signal never aggregates into a response. The next county over doesn't know. The state doesn't act. The national picture never forms. Patients pay the price.

The people who dismantled that infrastructure will be gone before we're done cleaning up what they broke. That is the particular cruelty of institutional destruction at scale — it outlasts the people who caused it. What we are seeing in the congenital syphilis numbers is not a coincidence. It is the arithmetic of defunded programs and eliminated divisions, expressed as sick babies.

What this means for you

Re-screen for syphilis in the third trimester, not just at first prenatal visit. Know the difference between your screening and confirmatory tests. Establish pharmacy relationships for Bicillin L-A now — before you need it — and document your contingency plan for when it's unavailable. If you are a patient: ask your provider about third-trimester syphilis screening. If you are a provider: the answer to shortage is preparation, not assumption.
