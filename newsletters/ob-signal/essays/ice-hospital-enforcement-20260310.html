The Hospital Was Supposed to Be Safe. That Is Being Taken From Us.

Dr. Yamicia Connor, MD, PhD, MPH  ·  March 10, 2026  ·  The Labora Signal

A woman with a high-risk pregnancy goes into labor at home. Not because she didn't know she needed a hospital. Because she calculated what walking into one might cost her and decided the risk was too high.

Physicians at HCMC in Minneapolis documented this pattern directly: patients with risky pregnancies avoiding prenatal care, going into labor alone, because of fear of immigration enforcement. ICE agents were reported refusing to leave exam rooms during private clinical encounters. The Minnesota Medical Association president said it out loud. The American Medical Association issued statements. None of it has enforcement power.

The clinical consequences are not abstract. Skipped prenatal care means undetected preeclampsia, undiagnosed gestational diabetes, missed fetal growth restriction — conditions where timing is everything. Delayed detection doesn't mean a harder case. It can mean a dead mother or a dead baby. And a woman delivering alone at home, without monitoring, faces obstetric emergencies that are measured in minutes: shoulder dystocia, cord prolapse, hemorrhage. The margin for surviving those without skilled intervention is narrow.

The hospital used to be one of the places society agreed was different. What happens outside waits at the door. That agreement is being dissolved — deliberately, by people who understand exactly what it does to a pregnant woman's willingness to seek care. This is not collateral damage. It is the mechanism.

Clinicians are being asked to absorb this alone. We are operating in an environment where we know enforcement is affecting care-seeking, we can see the downstream consequences arriving in front of us, and we have no institutional infrastructure to point to — no federal agency enforcing the HIPAA protections patients still technically have, no clear guidance on what we are required to do when an agent presents at the door of an exam room. We are being asked to be the last line of defense for patient privacy while being given nothing to defend it with.

What this produces is a documentation problem that will compound over time. The patients who avoid care don't appear in adverse outcome statistics as "avoided care due to enforcement fear." They appear as complications that arrived late, as deliveries without prenatal records, as maternal deaths with no warning signs on file because the warning signs were never captured. The mechanism is invisible in the data even as it is visible in the exam room.

What this means for you

You have the right to ask that clinical encounters occur without enforcement present. You cannot share patient information with agents without specific legal process — a warrant or subpoena, not a verbal request. Document every instance where enforcement presence affects care. That documentation is clinical, legal, and historical. If you are a patient: you have the right to receive care. Your medical information is protected by federal law. No verbal request from an agent is a legal requirement to disclose.
