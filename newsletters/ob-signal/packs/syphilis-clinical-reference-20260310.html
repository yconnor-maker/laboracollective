Surveillance Data: The Scope of the Surge

Congenital syphilis cases in the U.S. in 2022 (CDC MMWR). 937% increase since 2012.
+79%
Increase in primary and secondary syphilis among women of reproductive age, 2018–2022.
30yr
The 2022 congenital syphilis rate represents a 30-year high in the United States.
1943
Year penicillin was established as effective treatment for syphilis. The treatment we have had for 80+ years.

The states with the highest congenital syphilis rates include Southern and Western states where STI public health infrastructure has been most severely defunded. The CDC's 2024 Sexually Transmitted Infections Surveillance Report documents state-level trends; 2022 data is the most recent complete reporting year available.

Section 02

Clinical Staging: Recognition in Pregnant Patients

Stage Presentation Timing Clinical Notes
Primary Painless genital ulcer (chancre); may be internal/cervical and missed on exam 3–90 days post-exposure Often unrecognized. Chancre heals spontaneously — does not mean infection resolved.
Secondary Maculopapular rash (palms and soles), condylomata lata, mucous patches, systemic symptoms 2–12 weeks after primary Highly infectious. Rash may be subtle; palms/soles involvement is a key differentiator.
Early Latent No symptoms. Reactive serology only. Within 1 year of infection Detected only by screening. Treatable. Can still transmit to fetus.
Late Latent No symptoms. Reactive serology. >1 year or unknown duration Different dosing from early latent. Risk of fetal transmission present throughout pregnancy.
Congenital Early: rhinitis, hepatosplenomegaly, rash, anemia. Late: Hutchinson's triad, saber shins, saddle nose. In utero / at delivery Stillbirth, preterm birth, and infant death are serious outcomes. Cord blood may be tested.
Section 03

Screening Protocol: CDC 2021 STI Treatment Guidelines

Current Recommendation

Screen all pregnant patients at first prenatal visit, at 28 weeks, and at delivery in high-prevalence areas, or if risk factors present. Single first-visit screening is insufficient given the current surge.

Test Type Use
RPR / VDRL Nontreponemal (screening) Initial screen. High sensitivity, some false positives. Titer correlates with disease activity.
FTA-ABS / TPPA / TP-EIA Treponemal (confirmatory) Confirms positive nontreponemal. Remains reactive for life — does not distinguish active from treated.
Reverse Sequence Treponemal first, then nontreponemal Used by many labs; can detect early infection missed by RPR. Requires careful interpretation.

Interpreting discordant results: A positive treponemal test with a negative nontreponemal test requires clinical judgment: could be early infection, treated prior infection, or biologic false positive. Consult ID or maternal-fetal medicine when uncertain.

Section 04

Treatment: Protocols and Shortage Management

Standard of Care

Benzathine penicillin G (Bicillin L-A) is the ONLY antibiotic proven to adequately treat syphilis in pregnancy and protect the fetus. No alternative has equivalent efficacy. CDC and ACOG guidelines do not endorse amoxicillin, azithromycin, or doxycycline as substitutes in pregnancy.

StageDoseRouteNotes
Primary, Secondary, Early Latent Benzathine PCN G 2.4 million units ×1 IM Single dose for non-pregnant; same in pregnancy if <1 year duration.
Late Latent / Unknown Duration Benzathine PCN G 2.4 million units ×3 (one per week) IM Three-dose regimen. Do not substitute.
Penicillin Allergy in Pregnancy Penicillin desensitization Per protocol Desensitization is the recommended approach. Refer to allergist/ID. No oral alternative is equivalent.
Bicillin L-A Shortage: Current Status

Benzathine penicillin G has been on FDA shortage. Establish pharmacy relationships and confirm availability before a patient presents with positive syphilis serology. Pfizer manufactures Bicillin L-A; contact your pharmacy wholesaler for current availability. Document your contingency protocol in writing. When unavailable, CDC's STD Treatment Hotline (1-800-458-5231) provides clinical consultation.
